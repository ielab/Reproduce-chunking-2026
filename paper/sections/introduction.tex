\section{Introduction}

Dense retrieval systems have become the dominant paradigm for information retrieval, powering applications ranging from general web search to retrieval-augmented generation. A critical component of these systems is document chunking: the process of segmenting long documents into smaller, retrievable units. The chunking strategy directly impacts retrieval effectiveness: chunks that are too small may lack sufficient context, while chunks that are too large may dilute semantic signals and exceed embedding model capacity.

The traditional approach segments documents first and then encodes each chunk independently using a dense encoder. This \textit{pre-embedding chunking} paradigm has been the foundation for most retrieval systems, with variations primarily differing in how segmentation boundaries are determined: from simple fixed-size windows to sophisticated LLM-guided approaches~\cite{densex2024,lumberchunker2024}. Recently, \textit{contextualized chunking} methods have emerged, enabled by modern embedding models with extended context windows that support thousands of tokens beyond BERT's 512-token limit~\cite{e5,jina,bge}. Methods like Late Chunking~\cite{latechunking2024} exemplify this paradigm shift: they first encode the entire document to obtain contextual embeddings, then perform segmentation while preserving cross-chunk contextual information.


Simultaneously, segmentation methods themselves have evolved considerably. Beyond traditional fixed-size and sentence/paragraph-based approaches~\cite{}, recent work has introduced semantic boundary-based detection~\cite{propositions2024} and LLM-guided chunking~\cite{densex2024,lumberchunker2024}. However, these diverse methods are evaluated inconsistently across different retrieval scenarios, making systematic comparison challenging. For instance, proposition-based methods were evaluated using constrained-input models (typically 512-token limitation) on QA datasets~\cite{propositions2024}, while LumberChunker was evaluated on a fundamentally different task—single-book paragraph retrieval where each query is pre-scoped to a specific book—using commercial embedding APIs~\cite{lumberchunker2024}, and Late Chunking was evaluated on selected BEIR passage retrieval datasets for multi-document retrieval~\cite{latechunking2024}. This heterogeneity in evaluation settings span not only different embedding models, context window sizes, and datasets, but also fundamentally different retrieval tasks (single-document vs. multi-document retrieval), makes it impossible to determine whether performance differences stem from the chunking strategies themselves or from confounding factors.

These gaps motivate our research questions: \textbf{RQ1:} How do pre-embedding and contextualized chunking paradigms compare across different retrieval scenarios, and what are their trade-offs? \textbf{RQ2:} Do LLM-guided segmentation methods outperform simpler approaches when evaluated fairly across both retrieval paradigms? \textbf{RQ3:} How do different combinations of chunking order and segmentation method interact, and which combinations are most effective for specific scenarios?

To address these questions, we present a systematic framework that unifies existing chunking strategies along two key dimensions: (1) the \textit{order} of chunking and embedding operations (pre-embedding vs. contextualized chunking), and (2) the \textit{segmentation methods} (traditional fixed-size, sentence/paragraph-based, semantic boundary-based, and LLM-guided approaches). We reproduce and evaluate representative methods from each category across two distinct retrieval paradigms: single-document needle-in-a-haystack retrieval and multi-document web-scale retrieval using standard benchmarks such as MS MARCO and Natural Questions. This dual-paradigm evaluation enables us to identify which strategies generalize across scenarios versus those that excel only in specific settings.

Our contributions are threefold. First, we propose a comprehensive taxonomy that organizes the fragmented landscape of chunking strategies, providing a principled framework for understanding existing methods and identifying unexplored combinations. Second, we conduct the first systematic evaluation comparing pre-embedding and contextualized chunking paradigms alongside diverse segmentation strategies under controlled experimental conditions. Third, our empirical analysis reveals practical insights into the computational and effectiveness trade-offs of different approaches, providing actionable recommendations for practitioners. We release our complete implementation and evaluation framework to facilitate reproducibility and future research.
