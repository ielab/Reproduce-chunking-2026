\section{Introduction}

Dense retrieval systems, which represent queries and documents as low-dimensional vectors, have become a core part of modern information retrieval. They power a wide range of applications, from web search engines to advanced retrieval-augmented generation systems. A key challenge in using these systems is fitting long documents into the fixed-size input windows of embedding models. This requires \textbf{document chunking}: the process of dividing documents into smaller, meaningful units for indexing and retrieval. The choice of a chunking strategy is not just a preprocessing step; it is an important decision that greatly affects how well retrieval works. Chunks that are too small may not have enough context to answer a query, while oversized chunks can make it harder to find the relevant information and may be too large for the model to process.

Chunking has developed in two main ways: the method of segmentation and the approach to embedding. Traditionally, systems use a \textit{pre-embedding chunking} approach, where documents are first divided and each resulting chunk is then encoded on its own. Within this approach, segmentation methods range from simple fixed-size windows to more advanced techniques based on linguistic boundaries (e.g., sentences or paragraphs~\cite{para}), changes in meaning~\cite{propositions2024}, and even guidance from large language models (LLMs)~\cite{densex2024,lumberchunker2024}. More recently, the introduction of embedding models with longer context windows that support thousands of tokens~\cite{e5,jina,bge} has allowed for a new \textit{contextualized chunking} approach. Methods like Late Chunking~\cite{latechunking2024} are a good example of this change: they first encode a whole document to create context-aware embeddings and only then divide it, keeping useful information that connects the chunks.

Despite this rapid development, progress has been disorganized because there has been no standard way to evaluate these methods. New chunking strategies are often tested in different and unconnected ways, making it impossible to compare them directly. For example, proposition-based methods were evaluated on question-answering datasets using models with a 512-token limit~\cite{propositions2024}. In contrast, LLM-guided methods like LumberChunker were benchmarked on a single-book retrieval task using private embedding APIs~\cite{lumberchunker2024}, while Late Chunking was tested on standard BEIR passage retrieval benchmarks~\cite{latechunking2024}. These differences cover embedding models, datasets, and even the type of retrieval task (e.g., searching through many documents vs. a single document). As a result, it is unclear whether the differences in performance come from the actual strengths of the chunking strategies themselves or from these other factors in the experiments.

To address these gaps and provide a clear, evidence-based understanding of document chunking, we conduct a careful study guided by the following research questions:

\textbf{RQ1} \textit{How do different segmentation methods compare in standard multi-document retrieval?} We evaluate fixed-size, sentence/paragraph-based, semantic boundary-based, and LLM-guided chunking methods using traditional pre-embedding chunking on standard BEIR benchmarks.

\textbf{RQ2} \textit{Do segmentation methods generalize across retrieval paradigms?} We apply the same segmentation methods to single-document needle-in-a-haystack retrieval, testing whether the relative strengths observed in multi-document settings hold when the retrieval scope is constrained to individual documents.

\textbf{RQ3} \textit{Can contextualized chunking provide consistent improvements across different configurations?} We apply contextualized chunking to all segmentation methods from RQ1 across both retrieval paradigms from RQ2 to determine whether contextualized chunking provides universal benefits.

\textbf{RQ4} \textit{What factors determine when contextualized chunking outperforms pre-embedding approaches?} We implement Late Chunking as a representative contextualized method and systematically vary chunk size, overlap ratios, document types, and query characteristics to measure both retrieval effectiveness and computational overhead.

Through investigating these research questions, we (1) establish comprehensive baselines for segmentation methods in multi-document retrieval (\textbf{RQ1}), (2) identify which chunking strategies are robust across different retrieval paradigms (\textbf{RQ2}), (3) assess whether contextualized chunking provides universal improvements (\textbf{RQ3}), (4) uncover the key factors that determine when contextualized chunking justifies its computational overhead (\textbf{RQ4}), and (5) release our complete implementation and evaluation framework to facilitate reproducibility and future research.
