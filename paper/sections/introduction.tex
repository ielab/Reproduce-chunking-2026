\section{Introduction}

Dense retrieval systems have become the dominant paradigm for information retrieval, powering applications ranging from general web-search to retrieval-augmented generation. A critical component of these systems is document chunking: the process of segmenting long documents into smaller, retrievable units. The chunking strategy directly impacts retrieval effectiveness: chunks that are too small may lack sufficient context, while chunks that are too large may dilute semantic signals and exceed embedding model capacity.

The traditional approach, which we term \textit{chunk-then-embed}, segments documents first and then encodes each chunk independently using a dense encoder. This paradigm has been the foundation for most retrieval systems, with variations primarily differing in how segmentation boundaries are determined: from simple fixed-size windows to sophisticated semantic boundary detection based on LLMs~\cite{propositions,lumberchunker}. Recently, alternative paradigms have emerged that challenge this conventional pipeline, enabled by the long context input ability of modern embedding models, such as E5, Jina, and BGE that go beyond BERT's 512 token limit to support thousands of tokens~\cite{}. \textit{Contextual chunking} methods, exemplified by Late Chunking~\cite{latechunking}, reverse the traditional order: they first encode the entire document to obtain contextual embeddings, then perform segmentation while preserving cross-chunk contextual information. This shift from chunk-then-embed to embed-then-chunk represents a fundamental rethinking of the chunking process.
Simultaneously, the segmentation methods themselves have evolved considerably. Beyond basic fixed-size and sentence-based approaches, recent work has introduced semantic boundary detection (e.g. proposition-based segmentation~\cite{propositions}) and LLM-based chunking(e.g., LumberChunker~\cite{lumberchunker}.


Simultaneously, the segmentation methods themselves have evolved considerably. Beyond basic fixed-size and sentence-based approaches, recent work has introduced semantic boundary detection (e.g., proposition-based segmentation~\cite{propositions}, and LLM-guided chunking (e.g., LumberChunker~\cite{lumberchunker}), . However, these diverse methods are evaluated inconsistently across different retrieval scenarios, making systematic comparison challenging. For example, proposition-based methods were evaluated using constrained-input models (typically 512 token limitation) on QA datasets such as Natural Questions and SQuAD~\cite{propositions}, while LumberChunker was evaluated on a self-curated dataset GutenQA, where retrieval happens per-book and using commercial API embedding models (OpenAI)~\cite{lumberchunker}, and Late Chunking was evaluated on selected BEIR passage retrieval datasets using modern long-context models~\cite{latechunking}. This heterogeneity in evaluation settings spans different embedding models, context window sizes, datasets, and retrieval tasks makes it impossible to determine whether performance differences stem from the chunking strategies themselves or from confounding factors in the experimental setup.


These gaps motivate our research questions: \textbf{RQ1:} How do chunk-then-embed and contextual chunking paradigms compare across different retrieval scenarios, and what are the trade-offs between them? \textbf{RQ2:} Do semantic segmentation methods (e.g., LumberChunker) outperform simpler fixed-size approaches when evaluated fairly across both single-document and multi-document retrieval tasks? \textbf{RQ3:} How do different combinations of chunking paradigm and segmentation strategy interact, and which combinations are most effective for specific retrieval scenarios?

To address these questions, we present a systematic framework that unifies existing chunking strategies along two orthogonal dimensions: (1) the temporal ordering of chunking and embedding operations (chunk-then-embed vs. contextual chunking), and (2) the segmentation method employed (fixed-size, semantic boundary-based, and LLM-guided approaches). We reproduce and evaluate representative methods from each category, including LumberChunker and Late Chunking, across two distinct retrieval paradigms: single-document needle-in-haystack retrieval and multi-document web-scale retrieval using standard benchmarks such as MS MARCO and Natural Questions. This dual-paradigm evaluation enables us to identify which strategies generalize across scenarios versus those that excel only in specific settings.

Our contributions are threefold. First, we propose a comprehensive taxonomy that organizes the fragmented landscape of chunking strategies, providing a principled framework for understanding existing methods and identifying unexplored combinations. Second, we conduct the first systematic evaluation comparing chunk-then-embed and contextual chunking paradigms alongside diverse segmentation strategies under controlled experimental conditions across both retrieval paradigms. Third, our empirical analysis reveals practical insights into the computational and effectiveness trade-offs of different approaches, providing actionable recommendations for practitioners. We release our complete implementation and evaluation framework to facilitate reproducibility and future research.
