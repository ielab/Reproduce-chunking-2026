
\section{Benchmarking Document Chunking Strategies}
\label{sec:benchmarking_document_segmentation_strategies}

Document chunking translates raw text into retrievable units and therefore sits at the critical interface between corpus preparation and dense retrieval. To reason about the wide range of published chunkers, we organize the design space along two orthogonal dimensions: (1) \textbf{chunk boundary granularity}, i.e., how textual boundaries are detected, and (2) \textbf{embedding order}, i.e., whether chunking happens before or after contextual representations are produced. The taxonomy below provides common terminology for our experimental comparisons and highlights where new techniques diverge from conventional pipelines.

\subsection{Taxonomy of Chunking Strategies}

We divide chunking strategies into structure-preserving heuristics and semantically informed approaches. Both families can be instantiated with different hyper-parameters (window size, overlap, confidence thresholds), but they reflect distinct assumptions about how meaningful retrieval units should be constructed.

\subsubsection{Structure-Preserving Heuristics}
Heuristic chunkers rely on observable surface structure and have historically dominated production retrieval systems because they are deterministic, fast, and easy to parallelize~\ref{todo:heuristics-history}. We consider three representatives:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fixed-size windows} partition the document into contiguous spans of a preset token length, optionally with overlap to soften boundary effects. Their main appeal is hardware efficiency because index sharding and batch encoding are straightforward, but they may cut through coherent discourse units, creating embeddings that blend unrelated topics~\cite{multi_hop_retrieval}.
    \item \textbf{Paragraph boundaries} treat author-provided paragraph breaks as chunk anchors. This respects document-level structure with negligible preprocessing cost and often aligns with topical shifts in well-edited corpora such as news or scientific articles~\ref{todo:paragraph-retrieval}.
    \item \textbf{Sentence blocks} aggregate a small, fixed number of consecutive sentences. This yields finer-grained segments that are attractive for QA-style tasks where precise answer localization matters, though the shorter context can harm recall when evidence is distributed across a broader span~\ref{todo:sentence-qa}.
\end{itemize}

These heuristics illustrate the trade-off between computational efficiency and semantic completeness. Because they ignore discourse cohesion beyond surface cues, effectiveness can degrade when relevant information straddles boundary decisions, motivating more adaptive chunkers.

\subsubsection{Semantically Informed and LLM-Guided Strategies}
Recent work leverages contextual representations or large language models (LLMs) to place boundaries at semantic inflection points instead of predetermined offsets. The intuition is to preserve the internal coherence of each chunk so that dense retrievers operate on self-contained units~\ref{todo:semantic-splitting}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Semantic sentence chunking} computes similarity between adjacent sentences using lightweight embedding models and inserts a boundary when topical drift exceeds a threshold~\cite{greg_5_levels_of_text_splitting_nodate}. The resulting variable-length segments adapt to discourse structure while remaining automation-friendly.
    \item \textbf{Proposition-level chunking} decomposes text into minimal factual statements (``propositions'') via prompt-engineered LLMs~\cite{chen_dense_2024}. Each chunk aims to capture an atomic claim, which is beneficial for factoid retrieval but can explode the number of segments and increase indexing cost.
    \item \textbf{LLM-guided chunking (e.g., LumberChunker)} explicitly asks an LLM to mark coherent sections after reading the surrounding context~\cite{duarte_lumberchunker_2024}. This approach can exploit discourse cues that embeddings miss (narrative arcs, rhetorical structure), yet its cost scales with token limits and inference latency, constraining applicability to large corpora~\ref{todo:llm-cost}.
\end{itemize}

These strategies sacrifice some determinism for semantic fidelity. In our experiments we instantiate each chunker using publicly released implementations wherever possible to ensure comparability with prior reports~\ref{todo:impl-sources}.

\subsection{Pre-Embedding vs. Contextualized Chunking}

The second axis of our taxonomy concerns \textbf{when} chunking occurs relative to representation learning. Traditional \textit{pre-embedding} pipelines segment first, then encode each chunk independently~\ref{todo:pre-embedding-pipelines}. This design enables embarrassingly parallel encoding and straightforward inverted-file style indexing, but it strips away inter-chunk context because the encoder never observes more than one span at a time. When relevant evidence spans multiple segments, the resulting embeddings may be incomplete or inconsistent.

\textit{Contextualized chunking} (also called late chunking) inverts this order. Long-context encoders process the entire document (or large overlapping windows) to produce token-level embeddings that already incorporate global information~\cite{gunther_late_2025}. Afterward, chunk boundaries, whether fixed windows or LLM-derived spans, are applied to the contextualized tokens, and segment embeddings are aggregated through pooling or attention-based summarization~\ref{todo:contextual-pooling}. This preserves cross-span cues (e.g., entity co-reference, causal chains) while still exposing chunk-level vectors to downstream retrievers.

The choice between these paradigms affects computational budgets and memory footprints. Pre-embedding scales gracefully to web-scale corpora but can underperform when the optimal chunk size exceeds an encoder's maximum context length. Contextualized chunking mitigates that issue at the cost of longer forward passes, additional pooling logic, and more complex engineering around sliding windows for very long documents~\ref{todo:late-overhead}. Our evaluation therefore contrasts both orders under matched chunking policies to isolate when the additional expense is justified.

\subsection{Mismatch in Evaluation Protocols}

Prior work on chunking spans heterogeneous evaluation settings, complicating direct comparison of reported gains~\cite{densex2024, lumberchunker2024, latechunking2024}. Two paradigms dominate:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{In-document (needle-in-a-haystack) retrieval} restricts the search space to a single long document and measures how quickly a method surfaces the relevant passage. Datasets such as GutenQA stress precise localization, rewarding chunkers that preserve narrative continuity and suppress noisy overlap~\ref{todo:needle-benchmarks}.
    \item \textbf{In-corpus (multi-document) retrieval} evaluates ranking quality across a large, heterogeneous collection like MS MARCO or the BEIR suite~\cite{bajaj_ms_2018, thakur_beir_2021}. Here, scalability, indexing efficiency, and robustness to domain shifts become critical, sometimes favoring simpler chunk boundaries that align with batching constraints.
\end{itemize}

The literature often varies additional factors simultaneously, including embedding models, context-window limits, and negative sampling strategies, leaving open whether the observed improvements stem from the chunker itself or from confounding experimental conditions~\ref{todo:confounded-evals}. By reproducing representative methods under a single unified framework, we disentangle these influences and quantify which chunking decisions truly matter for each retrieval regime.
