Document chunking is a critical preprocessing step in dense retrieval systems, yet the design space of chunking strategies remains poorly understood. While the standard paradigm follows a chunk-then-embed approach—segmenting documents before encoding each chunk independently—recent methods have introduced contextual alternatives that embed first and chunk later (e.g., Late Chunking), preserving cross-chunk context in the resulting embeddings. Concurrently, numerous chunking strategies have been proposed, ranging from traditional fixed-size windows to more recent semantic boundary detection (e.g., LumberChunker) and proposition-based segmentation. However, these methods are evaluated inconsistently across different retrieval scenarios: some works focus exclusively on single-document retrieval tasks, while others evaluate only on multi-document collections, making direct comparison difficult.

This paper presents a systematic framework that unifies existing chunking strategies along two key dimensions: (1) the order of chunking and embedding operations (pre-embedding vs. contextual chunking), and (2) the segmentation methods themselves (fixed-size, semantic boundary-based, and LLM-guided approaches). We reproduce and evaluate representative methods from each category, including LumberChunker and Late Chunking, across two distinct retrieval paradigms: single-document needle-in-haystack retrieval and multi-document web-scale retrieval.
